Square · Banking · Debit Card

Increasing Checking Activation Through Instant Virtual Card Issuance

Square Checking signup required sellers to complete debit card ordering. If they dropped at that step, their account was never issued. Based on funnel and research insight, I advocated removing that requirement so sellers could receive a Checking account with an instant virtual debit card, choose how they wanted to spend, and order a physical card only if they needed one.

After redesigning Square Checking to better communicate banking value, I led this follow-on initiative to turn that insight into an activation model built on seller choice, not card fulfillment.

Situation

When sellers signed up for Square Checking, they could access banking features to manage finances, move money, and spend their Square balance.

We noticed a significant drop off when sellers reached the debit card ordering step. Sellers who skipped ordering a physical card were less likely to complete Square Checking activation.

The initial assumption was technical: if a seller skipped card ordering, should we simply create their Checking account anyway? Through research and funnel analysis, we uncovered a deeper issue. Some sellers were not skipping because they did not want Square Checking. They were skipping because they did not want a physical debit card.

Our activation experience had connected banking access to a physical card. Square Checking activation felt tied to ordering a Square Debit Card, which created friction for sellers who preferred digital wallets, did not carry physical cards, wanted immediate access before receiving a mailed card, or were unsure whether skipping the card meant losing access to their account.

The experience was optimized around card fulfillment instead of seller intent.

Task

Advocate for seller choice instead of a required debit card order.

Many sellers expected a modern banking experience where they could access funds immediately, use a virtual card, add their card to a digital wallet, and decide later if they wanted a physical card. The opportunity was not just removing a step. It was provisioning Checking with instant virtual card access and letting sellers choose their spending path.

Success meant increasing Square Checking completion, reducing abandonment at card ordering, improving confidence in virtual card access, and helping sellers start spending sooner without forcing a physical card order.

Action

From funnel analysis to a reframed activation model.

Discovery · Finding the real drop off

I analyzed the onboarding funnel and identified a pattern. Sellers reached the card ordering step but abandoned before completing activation. The key question became whether sellers were rejecting Square Checking or rejecting the requirement of a physical card.

Research and behavioral insights showed the second. The mental model we needed to support was "I want access to my money," not "I need a physical card."

Strategy · Seller choice over required card ordering

Based on that insight, I advocated that debit card ordering should not be required to complete Checking signup. I partnered with product management, engineering, research, analytics, and banking teams to define a model that provisioned the account with an instant virtual debit card while preserving the option to order a physical card.

Square Checking became the primary value moment. Sellers gained choice in how they spent: virtual card, digital wallet, or physical card later. The card was no longer the gate to banking access.

Delivery · Confidence at the card step

The redesigned experience gave sellers immediate digital access, a clear choice between physical and virtual card paths, and messaging that explained skipping the physical card did not mean losing access to Square Checking.

We surfaced digital card options at the moment sellers needed reassurance and aligned the flow with expectations from digital first financial products.

Exploration

What sellers expected from modern banking activation.

Exploration focused on four expectations that the card first model did not meet:

  • Access funds immediately without waiting for delivery
  • Use a virtual card as a complete spending path
  • Add a card to Apple Pay or another digital wallet
  • Order a physical card later if it fit their workflow

This reframing shifted design conversations from "how do we get sellers through card ordering" to "how do we help sellers choose how they want to spend."

Design

Move Checking account creation ahead of optional card ordering.

Before · Checking account created after card ordering

Sellers moved through account creation, banking signup, and identity verification before reaching debit card personalization and shipping. The Checking account was not created until the end of that flow. If a seller dropped during card ordering, they left without an account, a virtual card, or access to banking features.

After · Checking account created before optional card ordering

In the updated flow, the Checking account is provisioned during banking signup and identity verification, before sellers reach debit card ordering. At that point, physical card ordering becomes optional. Sellers can skip it, access a virtual debit card, and use banking features immediately, or order a physical card when they want one.

Key design decisions

  • Remove debit card ordering as a signup requirement. Based on research insight, I advocated provisioning Checking with a virtual card instead of gating the account on physical card fulfillment.
  • Give sellers choice in how they spend. Virtual card, digital wallet, and optional physical card ordering became parallel paths, not a single required step.
  • Introduce virtual card access at activation. Sellers could start spending immediately without waiting for mail delivery.

System Impact

Established a more flexible activation model across Banking.

  • Decoupled Checking activation from physical card fulfillment across onboarding flows
  • Virtual card and digital wallet patterns reusable in card issuance experiences
  • Shared language around seller intent and access to money applied beyond debit card ordering
  • Cross team alignment on activation metrics that reflected completion, not just card orders

Results

Sellers completed activation and started spending sooner.

The redesigned experience increased Square Checking activation, reduced drop off at the debit card step, grew virtual card adoption, and shortened time to first spend. Sellers who previously abandoned at card ordering could complete setup, receive a virtual debit card, and choose whether to order a physical card later.

Reflection

Activation issues are often symptoms of a product assumption.

The challenge was not that sellers did not want Square Checking. The challenge was that requiring debit card ordering defined access too narrowly. Advocating for seller choice, instant virtual card provisioning, and optional physical card ordering created a more flexible experience that helped sellers start using their money sooner.

This work also reinforced how follow on initiatives benefit from prior context. After improving how sellers understood Checking value, the next barrier was clearer: the card step was filtering out sellers who were ready to bank digitally. Fixing that required owning the card experience, not just optimizing a funnel step.
