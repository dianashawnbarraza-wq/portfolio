Heal · Healthcare · Patient & Provider

Preventative care with data-driven patient health monitoring

Situation

Between visits, doctors were blind.

Heal provided on-demand, same-day doctor house calls, bringing care into patients’ homes rather than treating every visit as a one-off appointment. Our ambition was bigger: primary, preventative care delivered by Heal doctors, not episodic urgent visits.

The obstacle was visibility. A doctor saw a patient for an hour at home, then nothing until the next call. For adults 65 and older living with chronic conditions like hypertension and diabetes, the moments that matter most happen in that gap: the readings that drift, the medication that stops working, the early signal of an acute episode. Remote monitoring data technically existed in the system, but it lived behind notifications with no way to act on exceptions inline. Without a durable view into patient health over time, chronic conditions were harder to manage, repeat engagement suffered, and Heal could not demonstrate the preventative value Medicare required.

Task

Design an end-to-end patient health monitoring experience for physicians, against a hard growth target.

As Design DRI, I partnered across product, engineering, and clinical teams to define how monitoring would work for the doctors recommending it, the patients living with chronic conditions, and the business model behind recurring care.

We set a concrete goal: increase Heal’s repeat patient rate from 39% to 45% by introducing patient monitoring, while building the clinical and product foundation Medicare approval required.

Constraints:

  • Clinical safety. A missed threshold alert is not a UX bug, it is a patient outcome. Alert visibility could not depend on a doctor remembering to check a notification tray.
  • One month. The MVP design had to be validated with practicing physicians inside a single month, which shaped every process decision below.
  • High-volume practice days. Doctors reviewing monitoring data between house calls needed one-glance comprehension, not exploration.
  • Regulatory bar. Medicare’s chronic care requirements set the floor for documentation, review cadence, and care continuity. The workflow had to satisfy them by default, not by discipline.

Action

Discovery with clinicians, then rapid prototyping with a standing doctor panel.

Discovery · Arriving at an ideal patient journey

I began with an interview with the Head of Medicine, then worked alongside a project manager to establish a doctor panel for weekly feedback sessions.

Together we answered two critical questions for the MVP: how and when a doctor would recommend monitoring to a patient predisposed to or living with a condition, and which conditions to target first. Working with the panel and engineering, we co-created a patient journey for Medicare patients with chronic conditions, mapping when data collection, physician review, and intervention should happen across the care cycle.

Prototyping · Information architecture and data visualization

I designed an initial physician experience focused on information architecture, data visualization, and the panel’s core request: medication and patient history in one place.

Early feedback was positive, with clear gaps to close. Doctors needed to contact patients directly, manage how long monitoring would run, and interact with charts that surfaced detail on demand rather than static summaries. I iterated through weekly panel sessions, tightening workflows for clinical decision-making while keeping every screen scannable during high-volume practice days.

Design

From a schedule-first tool to a monitoring-first morning briefing.

The existing physician app centered on daily logistics: appointments, patient lookup, and chart review. Monitoring data lived behind a notification bell, competing with every other ping for attention.

The redesign rests on one decision: in clinical work, an alert is not an interruption to the doctor’s day, it is the doctor’s day. So threshold alerts moved out of notifications and onto the surface where doctors already start each morning. The new Overview is a briefing that answers the question every physician asks first, which of today’s patients needs my attention, by placing inline monitoring alerts, upcoming appointments, and patient status in a single prioritized view.

Before · Schedule-centric workflow

  • Appointments: daily schedule
  • Patient detail: overview, history, prescriptions

After · Monitoring-first briefing

  • Overview: morning briefing with inline monitoring alerts instead of buried notifications
  • Patient detail: monitoring tab with trend charts, device context, and prescriptions, so a doctor can read weeks of readings in seconds and judge signal against device noise

Results

Physician visibility unlocked preventative care at home.

  • Repeat patient rate increased to 47%, exceeding the 45% goal, while acute episodes fell and health outcomes improved
  • With monitoring paid by Medicare, the program was free for patients and drove recurring net margins of more than $4M per year
  • Unnecessary prescriptions dropped 39% for monitored patients, because doctors adjusted care based on trends instead of single readings
  • Monitoring capabilities directly supported Heal’s Medicare approval, the unlock that made ongoing primary care at home economically viable
