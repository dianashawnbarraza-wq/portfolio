Question 2 · BFF — Better, Faster, Fewer
Tell me about a time you prioritized ruthlessly.
Two strong stories. Flyhomes is the sharpest — the numbers are dramatic and the ruthlessness is visible. Biller pause is your backup when they want a "when did you decide not to build" answer.
Primary · Flyhomes tour booking
"During the 2020 housing boom at Flyhomes, home buyers needed to win fast. In a market where houses were closing over asking with cash offers in 48 hours, every hour of pre-offer friction cost a buyer a real chance at a home. And the trickiest part of the pre-offer stage was touring. The workflow was manual: a buyer wanted to see a house, the tour coordinator emailed a partner listing agent, waited for a response, negotiated a time, confirmed it back. Tour confirmation was averaging five hours. In a 48-hour market, five hours can be the difference between seeing the house and losing it. I owned the internal tools that tour coordinators used. I sat with them, mapped their journey, and identified the specific job the design had to do: compress the confirmation loop from a manual back-and-forth to an automated booking. I ruled out anything that didn't touch that job. I designed a coordinator workflow that used API calls into partner scheduling systems to book tours directly — no email back-and-forth, no waiting. Confirmation dropped from five hours to thirty minutes. Buyers were touring more homes with less overhead. Homes toured before an offer dropped from 40 to 18 — because our buyers were more confident, faster. And tour support calls dropped 44 percent — the workflow was self-serve enough that coordinators didn't need help. That was ruthless prioritization: I didn't build a better inbox, I built the thing that killed the inbox for that use case. Understanding the job to be done let me cut everything that wasn't compression."

Question 3 · SBWH — Strong Beliefs, Weakly Held
Tell me about a time you changed your mind.
Card decoupling. Belief → new information → fast update → number.
"When I joined the Cards team, one of my first projects was the end of the Banking sign-up flow. Sellers were dropping off after identity verification — they didn't want to order a physical card and were exiting before completing sign-up. My first instinct was strong: sellers should not be able to skip card ordering. The card was the product. I believed requiring it would drive commitment. Then I sat down with engineering to understand the technical model. Card ordering wasn't required to open a Checking account at all. The account and the card were completely decoupable. A seller could open Checking and immediately access a virtual card — add it to Apple Wallet, start spending the same day, order the physical card later when ready. That conversation changed my position completely. I had been designing around a constraint that wasn't real. And once I updated, I realized the decoupled experience was actually more respectful — they hadn't deposited a dollar yet. Forcing a physical card at that moment was the wrong ask. We shipped the decoupled experience. Activation lifted 23 percent. Virtual card adoption increased 38 percent. I had a strong belief going in. Better information changed it. I try to update fast when that happens."

Question 4 · FUEL — Feedback Uplifts Every Level
Tell me about feedback that was hard to hear.
Two stories. Copy feedback is your primary — personal, craft-focused, honest. Overview vision is your secondary — about sharing feedback early and moving forward together.
Primary · The copy feedback
"A PM told me I was relying too much on instructional copy — educational modals, secondary explanations, timing notes about how money movement worked. Their read was that I was compensating for product gaps with words instead of fixing the gaps. That was hard to hear because I genuinely believed I was being helpful. I thought thoroughness was respect for the seller. The more I explained, the better they'd understand. But when I looked at session data, sellers weren't reading the copy. They were skipping past it or abandoning the flow entirely. The words weren't helping them move forward — they were slowing them down. What made this land even harder was that it wasn't a one-off — I had the same instinct on vendor acceptance in Bill Pay. I thought giving Bill Pay users the ability to customize the notification email would build trust with vendors. What that would have actually done was create a moment of pause in the flow when what the vendor needed was a better way to accept the payment on their end. Both times, I was designing for warmth when the user needed friction removed. I made a deliberate shift. I started asking: what would this screen communicate if it had no copy at all? If the answer is nothing, the design isn't done. The product has to do the heavy lifting. Words are what you add when the design isn't clear enough yet. If a seller needs a paragraph to understand a feature, the feature needs to be redesigned — not re-explained."

"Heal's mission was to bring primary care into the home — closing care gaps, improving preventive measures, and reducing white coat syndrome for patients with chronic illnesses. App Critique · 20 minutes · Cathleen Jia
Ground → Scope → Walk → Prioritize → Hand back.
Start with the user, not with the intro. Cathleen has already been briefed on you — go straight to the product.
⚠️ Don't do this at the start
Don't reintroduce yourself. Cathleen has your resume. Culture panel already heard your bio. Bio at the start signals nerves.
Don't ask for a brief. The prompt IS the site. If you must clarify, keep it to one sentence: "Any particular user or flow you'd like me to focus on, or should I pick the critical path?"
Do land on the product immediately. Ground in the user first. That's the senior move.
1
Ground in the user
2 min · Rubric-critical: this is the top scored dimension
Name the user, their job to be done, their pain points, and the business model. Every observation you make later will tie back to this. Don't just describe who the user is — describe what they're feeling when they arrive.
"Let me ground us in the user first. This product serves [primary user: e.g., a hungry office worker at 12:30 on a Tuesday]. Their job is [get lunch fast, within budget, quality high enough to not regret]. Their pain points are [decision fatigue across too many options, fee surprise at checkout, uncertainty about wait time]. The business model is a three-sided marketplace — restaurant, driver, customer — which means the platform is constantly balancing three interests that aren't fully aligned. That tension shows up in the UI, and I'll flag it as we go."
2
Invite collaboration + declare your path
1 min · This is scored too — treat her as a collaborator
Cathleen's rubric explicitly rewards designers who engage the interviewer. Ask her a low-cost question and announce your walking path.
"Before I walk, does that framing match how you'd frame the user, or would you sharpen it? [Listen.] Great. I'm going to walk the critical path — homepage discovery through restaurant selection to cart. If you want me to detour anywhere or zoom in on a specific surface, just call it out."
3
Walk with narrative structure
12 min · Narrative logic beats a list of gripes
Don't jump around. Move through the flow with clear transitions: "the first thing I notice… which sets up… which is where the friction really appears…" For each moment: what you observe → why it matters to the user's job → why the business likely made this call → what you'd test.
"What I see here is [observation]. For a user trying to [job], this either helps them or costs them [X]. This decision probably exists because [revenue reason / operational constraint / tradeoff]. If I owned this, I'd test [specific change] and watch [metric] against baseline [Y]."
🤝
Invite her in mid-walk: "I'm curious how you'd rank these — is [X] the friction you'd focus on or does [Y] read as bigger?"
✨
Name what's working, not just what isn't. "What I want to name that's working — the [X] pattern is doing a lot of quiet lifting." Critics who only tear down look junior.
4
Prioritize with rationale
3 min · Two opportunities, ranked, with tests + metrics
Close with two changes in priority order. Say why one is bigger. Every recommendation gets a test and a metric — this is your signature move.
"If I could change two things in priority order: one, [biggest change] — because it sits on the critical path and would move [outcome metric]. I'd test [A] against baseline [Y]. Two, [second change] — it's smaller in impact but higher confidence. I'd test [C] and measure [D]."
5
Hand back the mic
2 min · Partnership, not performance
Turn it back explicitly. Ask what she wants to explore further.
"That's my read on the critical path. What's a flow or moment you'd want me to go deeper on? Or is there something you saw differently?"

UI wireframe best practices
Their brief explicitly asks for a visualization — wireframe, flow, or interaction concept. You will need to draw something. Here's how to make it look intentional in FigJam under pressure.
📐
Draw one screen well, not five screens poorly. A single annotated wireframe with labeled regions beats a whole flow of empty boxes.
📱
Frame your canvas first. Draw a phone rectangle (roughly 375×812 proportions) or a browser window. It orients everyone.
🏷
Label everything as you draw. Header, primary action, list row, empty state. Names carry the meaning; the boxes are scaffolding.
🧭
Show hierarchy with size, not detail. Bigger boxes = more important. Don't try to draw pixel-perfect chrome — you're proving a concept.
💬
Annotate decisions in text next to the screen. "Why this here" beats "how it looks." Sticky notes with rationale > pretty wireframes.
↔️
Use arrows for state changes and flows. "User taps this → sees that." A three-frame flow with arrows tells a story.
🎯
Prioritize the "hero moment." Pick the ONE screen or interaction that carries your idea. Draw that first. Everything else supports it.
🗣
Narrate every stroke. "I'm putting the primary action here because…" Silent sketching is invisible thinking, and this is a scored rubric item.

The 5-stage decision map · use this in the whiteboard
Discover — "Where do I start?" · too many options · AI: conversational search
Evaluate — "Which is best?" · endless comparison · AI: personalized recs with explanations
Decide — "Am I right?" · low confidence · AI: tradeoff summaries + confidence signals
Purchase — "What's next?" · long forms, unclear pricing · AI: autofill, pricing clarity
Post — "Did I choose well?" · no reassurance · AI: next-step guidance, reminders
The universal journey map
Applies to any marketplace they hand you. Draw a version of this fast in FigJam — emotion on top, pain point below. The Decide stage (card 4) is where AI does its best work and where the confidence gap lives.
1 · Arrive
Open the app
"Where do I even start?"
😐Emotion: Neutral, slightly hopeful, low commitment
⚠️Pain: No context yet — the app doesn't know why they're here today
2 · Discover
See the options
"How many of these are there?"
😳Emotion: Mild overwhelm — the number of choices registers immediately
⚠️Pain: Flat list, everything looks similar, no sense of what's actually good
3 · Evaluate
Compare a few
"Which of these is actually best?"
😤Emotion: Mounting fatigue — bouncing between tabs/cards to compare manually
⚠️Pain: Comparable data isn't normalized (price vs. quality vs. speed, apples to oranges)
4 · Decide ⭐
Commit to one
"Am I making the right choice?"
😰Emotion: Anxiety spike — this is the highest-stakes emotional moment in the journey
⚠️Pain: Low confidence, no one is explaining why this option fits — the single biggest AI opportunity
5 · Transact
Complete the booking
"What happens next?"
😬Emotion: Relief mixed with second-guessing — did the price change, was the fee always there?
⚠️Pain: Long forms, surprise fees revealed late, unclear confirmation
6 · Follow-up
Live with the choice
"Did I choose well?"
🙂Emotion: Hope for validation — wants the choice reinforced or corrected fast
⚠️Pain: Silence after commitment. No reassurance, no next step, no easy path if it goes wrong

Draw all 6 stages fast in FigJam, left to right — even just boxes with a one-word emotion above and a pain point below each. Circle stage 4, Decide. The Preference Prompt · Safe bet, ships fast
Discover fix

Shown once to new-to-city users after they drop their pin. Three quick taps: cuisine mood, budget, dine-in vs fast. Surfaces 3 matched restaurants with a one-line "why this fits." Compresses Discover → Evaluate → Decide by roughly half.

Targets
Discover-stage fatigue
Metric
Time-to-restaurant-selection for new-to-city users, against today's baseline. Secondary: first-session order completion rate.

Weakness: front-loaded. Doesn't touch the in-menu anxiety you diagnosed at Decide. Fine as a starter, not the full answer.

B
The Confidence Layer · The one that actually solves Decide
Your pick

A lightweight "why this is a good pick" signal that follows the user from restaurant card all the way into the menu at the item level. Not a quiz — synthesized proof from real order data. "Most ordered by first-timers in this neighborhood." "9 of 10 people who ordered ramen here also got the gyoza."

Targets
Decide-stage anxiety · the peak you circled
Metric
Restaurant menu-open → order-placed conversion, against baseline. Secondary: time-in-menu as a proxy for hesitation. This is the number you'd pre-register before shipping.

Why this is the pick: it reaches the exact moment you diagnosed as the anxiety peak, not just the upstream problem. It's your BFF move — one focused bet on the highest-impact stage.

C
The Concierge · Most ambitious, AI-native
Future bet

A single conversational entry point: "What are you in the mood for?" Free text or chips. Returns 3 restaurants with one dish highlighted from each and a plain-language reason. Collapses Discover → Evaluate → Decide into one interaction. User never sees a wall of 200 restaurants.

Targets
The whole journey, redesigned around AI
Metric
First-session order completion for new-to-city users. Plus qualitative: does the recommendation feel trustworthy? Would need usability testing before rollout — that's part of the sequencing pitch.

The pitch: "Not where I'd start, but where I'd want this to evolve toward. I'd validate the confidence-signal pattern in B before betting the whole quarter on this."

If Cathleen asks "which would you pick?"
"If I had to pick one, B — the confidence layer. It's the only one of the three that actually reaches into the moment I flagged as the peak of the anxiety, not just the upstream restaurant-selection problem. A is safer to ship but only solves half of what I diagnosed. C is where I'd want this to evolve — genuinely AI-native — but I wouldn't bet the whole quarter on it without validating the smaller confidence-signal pattern first. Line to land
The safe answer solves the upstream problem. The strong answer solves the peak. The ambitious answer replaces the whole journey — but I'd sequence, not skip.
