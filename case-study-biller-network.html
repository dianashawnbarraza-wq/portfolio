Tackling the Cold Start in Bill Pay

Situation

Active users had stalled. Bill Pay had to prove product-market fit.

Square Bill Pay let sellers pay vendors and bills from the Square ecosystem, and growth had gone quiet. I went looking for why in two directions at once: a funnel analysis of where sellers dropped out of the product, and discovery research into where their bills actually lived.

Discovery answered first, and the answer was blunt: 80% of recurring bills, utilities, telecom, insurance, were being paid outside Square. The key insight underneath that number was understanding how sellers receive bills and where they manage them. Bill paying was a fragmented experience, scattered across biller websites, mail piles, and inboxes, and most bills were already arriving by email.

Task

Find where the funnel actually broke, then remove that step rather than improve it.

Action

The funnel said it plainly: "Add a vendor" lost 46% of sellers in a single step.

Step Completion Notes
Start a bill payment 100% Baseline
Add a vendor 54% 46% drop-off — flagged
Enter payment details 38% Ordinary step-over-step falloff
Schedule payment 32% Ordinary step-over-step falloff

Add a vendor lost more sellers than every other step in the funnel combined. The two steps after it decline too, down to 38% and 32%, but that's ordinary step-over-step falloff, not a signal, and it wasn't where I spent design attention. One step was the problem: sellers abandoned at the exact moment the product asked them to reconstruct vendor details they didn't have on hand.

The reframe: don't improve the vendor step, remove it. Two stages, sequenced by effort and impact, each shipped and measured against the Add-a-vendor conversion rate before committing to the next. Leading metrics shifted from completion rates to that conversion number and to sellers paying 2+ bills a month, the retention signal. Payment volume stayed the north star.

Design

Baseline 54%. OCR shipped first and did double duty. Email forwarding shipped next.

Baseline · Manual entry

List → blank bill form → vendor search → full vendor build. Four steps before a payment could move.

Stage 1 · Shipped · OCR + auto-created vendor + AI category

Scan any bill and OCR extracts the fields: biller, amount, due date. But it goes further: the vendor auto-creates from the scan, no search, no form. And a second AI moment lands in the same flow: Bill Pay looks up the vendor and applies a suggested category automatically. Two quiet AI assists inside a flow sellers already trusted, not a chatbot bolted to the side. This stage alone moved Add-a-vendor conversion from 54% to 62%.

Stage 2 · Shipped · Email forwarding (zero UI)

Discovery showed most bills already arrive by email. Sellers forward a bill to a dedicated address and it ingests itself: parsed, matched, ready to schedule. This stage pushed conversion to 69%.

The last piece · Scoped, paused

I identified the Paymentus API and scoped the biller-network integration: intent-first entry, prefilled vendor and due date from a national network. The project was paused before build when it lost funding priority. The two shipped stages had already moved conversion from 54% to 69%, which made doubling down on them the defensible call while the network waits its turn.

The design judgment: two cheap stages proved the model before the most expensive one needed a resourcing decision. Removing a step beats improving it, every time.

Results

Removing the step beat improving it.

Add-a-vendor conversion moved from 54% to 62% after OCR and auto-created vendors shipped, then to 69% after email forwarding removed the step entirely for most bills. Payment volume grew approximately 23%, the north star metric. The biller network remains scoped and paused, on the roadmap for when it's resourced.

Reflection

The shipped results made pausing the network a decision, not a setback.

The constraint was never the payment flow. It was one step, Add a vendor, that lost 46% of sellers outright. Leading with the cheapest, highest-leverage fixes first, OCR with AI-assisted vendor creation, then a zero-UI ingestion channel, meant conversion moved twice before the most expensive piece needed a funding call. That sequencing is what made the pause defensible instead of a stall.
