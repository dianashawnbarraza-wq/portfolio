Executive Summary

Over the course of a multi-quarter product cycle, I led end-to-end product design efforts across Point of Sale and web to improve seller understanding, activation, and engagement with Square Checking.

By combining customer research, behavioral data, experimentation, and cross-functional alignment, we redesigned key onboarding and account experiences that helped close activation gaps and increase engagement among newly enrolled sellers.

  • Increased activation among organically enrolled sellers from 54% → 59%
  • Activation among onboarding-acquired users improved substantially toward our target, moving from approximately 32% toward the high-40% range
  • Reduced onboarding drop-off after identity verification
  • Increased engagement with core banking features including debit card usage, account management, and deposits

Situation

Square Checking offered sellers a business bank account tightly integrated with their existing Square ecosystem.

As enrollment expanded through seller onboarding, activation rates lagged behind expectations. Many sellers successfully signed up for Checking but failed to complete key actions needed to realize value from the product.

The team knew activation was critical because activated sellers were significantly more likely to engage with Square Banking, use their debit card, receive deposits, and remain active customers over time.

Opportunity

Through research and behavioral analysis, we identified three key issues holding back activation.

1

Sellers didn't understand they had a bank account

Many sellers completed onboarding but did not fully realize they had enrolled in Square Checking.

2

The value of Checking was unclear

Critical actions such as finding account numbers, managing debit cards, receiving deposits, and sending money were difficult to discover.

3

Key activation actions were buried

Important onboarding steps and banking tools were hidden behind navigation patterns that sellers rarely explored.

These issues created friction immediately after signup and contributed to lower activation rates among onboarding-acquired sellers.

My Role

I led the initiative from discovery through launch as Design Directly Responsible Individual (DRI).

My responsibilities included:

  • Defining the experience strategy
  • Conducting and synthesizing research
  • Identifying activation opportunities
  • Partnering with data science to understand drop-off behavior
  • Facilitating alignment across Banking, Growth, and Platform teams
  • Designing and validating solutions
  • Supporting experimentation and launch planning

This work spanned multiple surfaces across Point of Sale and web and required balancing customer needs, technical constraints, and business objectives.

Actions

Four strategic workstreams reframed the problem from increasing signups to increasing successful activation.

1 · Identified activation as the primary growth lever

I partnered with Product and Data Science to map the seller journey and identify where activation broke down. We found that a significant percentage of sellers completed identity verification but never fully activated their account.

This reframed the problem from "increase signups" to "increase successful activation."

Image · Funnel analysis / journey map
2 · Clarified the value of Checking

Research showed sellers often viewed Checking as another Square feature rather than a business bank account. To address this, I redesigned key account surfaces to:

  • Reinforce account ownership
  • Surface account and routing information
  • Highlight debit card access
  • Improve visibility of deposit and payment capabilities
  • Create stronger mental models around banking functionality

3 · Prioritized core banking actions

I reorganized the experience around the actions sellers most frequently needed:

  • Access account information
  • Activate and manage debit cards
  • Receive deposits
  • Send and spend money

Rather than introducing additional features, we simplified the experience and reduced friction around existing behaviors.

4 · Aligned multiple teams around a shared vision

Because Checking touched several product areas, success depended on coordination across teams. I facilitated workshops, created experience principles, and aligned stakeholders around a unified activation strategy.

This helped prioritize work across multiple roadmaps while maintaining a consistent seller experience.

Reflection

This project reinforced that activation is often a product understanding problem rather than a feature problem.

By clarifying value, simplifying key workflows, and aligning teams around customer needs, we were able to improve activation without introducing significant new functionality.

More importantly, the work established a foundation for future growth initiatives across the Square Banking ecosystem.

"Activation is often a product understanding problem rather than a feature problem."

— Key takeaway from this initiative
